Next Steps.

You've got a site online. Now put your own domain on it, find out who's actually visiting, and give people a way to reach you — all still free, and still with no server to run.

Time About an hour, plus waiting on DNS.

A domain nameRoughly $10–20/year from any registrar — Namecheap, Porkbun, Cloudflare, GoDaddy. Any of them work.

Put your own domain on it

Right now your site lives at username.github.io. Here's how to make it answer at a name you own. The order matters — do DNS first, so the name resolves before GitHub goes looking for it.

1

Find your DNS settings

Log into wherever you bought the domain and find the DNS or DNS Records page. You're looking for a list of records with types like A, CNAME, and TXT.

You're not moving anything. You're just editing which servers the name points at.

2

Delete the placeholder records first

Registrars add their own records automatically so a freshly bought domain shows a parking page. If you leave them in, your visitors get the parking page part of the time instead of your site — which looks like a random, impossible-to-reproduce outage.

Delete any existing A record on @, and any existing CNAME on www. Leave everything else alone — especially MX records, which carry your email.

Watch for: records named things like "Parked", "WebsiteBuilder", or "Domain Forwarding". Those are the ones that fight with your site.
3

Add GitHub's records

Five records. The four IP addresses are GitHub's public servers — everyone uses the same four, so these aren't secret and you can copy them exactly.

TypeNameValue
A@185.199.108.153
A@185.199.109.153
A@185.199.110.153
A@185.199.111.153
CNAMEwwwusername.github.io

Swap username.github.io for your actual repo name. The four A records send yourdomain.com to GitHub; the CNAME does the same for www.yourdomain.com.

Now wait. DNS changes take anywhere from a few minutes to a couple of hours to spread. Grab a coffee before the next step.

4

Tell GitHub about it

In your repo: Settings → Pages → Custom domain. Type yourdomain.com and hit Save. GitHub runs a DNS check and shows a green tick when it can see your records.

Don't hand-edit the CNAME file. Saving here creates a file called CNAME in your repo automatically. Let this box manage it. If you edit that file directly, the two fall out of sync and the domain breaks in a confusing way.
5

Turn on HTTPS — but wait for the tick

Under the domain box is an Enforce HTTPS checkbox. GitHub gives you a free certificate, but it takes time to issue — sometimes an hour.

Leave the box alone until it stops being greyed out. Tick it too early and visitors get scary security warnings.

Then test both: yourdomain.com and www.yourdomain.com. Both should land on your site over https://.

If you ever change domains — read this

This one bites people, and the failure is worse than a broken link. Say you move from an old name to a new one and update your GitHub custom domain. Job done, right?

Not quite. Your old domain's DNS is still pointing at GitHub — but no repo claims that name anymore. It's now aimed at GitHub's servers with nobody home. That's called a dangling domain.

Why it matters: anyone with a GitHub account can add your abandoned domain to their repo and serve whatever they want at your old address. Everyone following an old link, bookmark, or business card lands on a stranger's page under your name. This is a well-known attack, not a hypothetical.

So whenever you retire a domain, deal with it in the same sitting. You have two clean options:

  • Leave the DNS pointing at GitHub. GitHub automatically redirects any domain aimed at your Pages site to your current custom domain — so old links keep working, with free HTTPS, for zero effort. Usually the better choice.
  • Or strip it entirely. Delete those four A records and the www CNAME at your registrar, then set up domain forwarding to the new name instead.

What you must never do is leave the records pointing at GitHub with no repo of yours claiming the name.

Belt and braces: in Settings → Pages there's a verified domains feature at the account level. Turn it on. It permanently stops anyone else claiming your domains on GitHub Pages. Five minutes, once, forever.

See who's visiting

Cloudflare Web Analytics is free, needs no cookies, and doesn't require you to move your DNS to Cloudflare. It's a small script that reports from the visitor's browser.

1

Add your site

Make a free Cloudflare account. In the dashboard sidebar find Web Analytics, then Add a site and enter yourdomain.com. It hands you a snippet with a token in it.

2

Paste it into every page

Drop this just before the closing </head> tag, with your own token swapped in:

<script defer src="https://static.cloudflareinsights.com/beacon.min.js"
  data-cf-beacon='{"token": "YOUR_TOKEN_HERE"}'></script>
Every page, not just the homepage. The single most common mistake is putting it only on index.html. Miss it on your contact page and you'll never know how many people got that far — which is exactly the number you want.
3

Read it in the right place

Stats live under Web Analytics in the sidebar — an account-level page, not inside a domain's settings.

If a domain's own Analytics tab looks empty, that's normal. Your traffic goes from your registrar straight to GitHub and never passes through Cloudflare's network, so there's nothing for it to report. The Web Analytics page, fed by that browser script, is your real source.

Read the numbers honestly

  • Recent data is exact; older data is estimated. Roughly the first week is counted fully, then it's sampled and scaled up. Treat old figures as directional.
  • For a spike — a shoutout, a viral post — check within the first 24 hours for the cleanest read.
  • It's deleted after about six months. If you care about year-over-year, screenshot your monthly numbers now and then.

Let people contact you

A plain mailto: link works, but it dumps people into an email client and you get scattered, half-finished messages. A real form asks the questions you actually need answered.

Normally forms need a server. These services are the way around that: your form posts to them, they email you the result.

1

Get an access key

Sign up at a form service (Web3Forms needs no account — enter your email and it sends a key straight back). You get a long string that routes submissions to your inbox.

This key goes in your public HTML, so treat it as semi-public. It can only send mail to the address you registered, so it's not dangerous — but don't reuse it as a password anywhere, and rotate it if you get spammed hard.
2

Build the form

A working starting point. The hidden fields do the real work:

<form id="contact">
  <input type="hidden" name="access_key" value="YOUR_ACCESS_KEY">
  <input type="hidden" name="subject" value="New enquiry from yourdomain.com">

  <!-- honeypot: hidden from people, bots fill it in -->
  <input type="checkbox" name="botcheck" style="display:none">

  <label>Your name
    <input type="text" name="name" required></label>

  <label>Email
    <input type="email" name="email" required></label>

  <label>How did you hear about me?
    <select name="heard_via">
      <option>Search</option>
      <option>Friend / word of mouth</option>
      <option>Social media</option>
      <option>Other</option>
    </select></label>

  <label>Message
    <textarea name="message" rows="5" required></textarea></label>

  <button type="submit">Send</button>
  <p id="result"></p>
</form>
That honeypot line is worth keeping. It's invisible to humans, but automated spam bots fill in every field they find. The service silently bins anything with it ticked. Costs you one line, kills most junk.
3

Submit without leaving the page

Without this, people get bounced to a bare third-party thank-you page. This keeps them on your site:

<script>
document.getElementById('contact').addEventListener('submit', async (e) => {
  e.preventDefault();
  const result = document.getElementById('result');
  result.textContent = 'Sending…';
  try {
    const res = await fetch('https://api.web3forms.com/submit', {
      method: 'POST',
      headers: { 'Content-Type': 'application/json' },
      body: JSON.stringify(Object.fromEntries(new FormData(e.target)))
    });
    if (!res.ok) throw new Error();
    e.target.reset();
    result.textContent = 'Thanks — I'll get back to you soon.';
  } catch {
    result.textContent = 'Something went wrong. Please email me directly.';
  }
});
</script>
4

Close the loop

Two numbers, two places. Submissions land in your inbox and the form service's dashboard. How many people reached the form is in Cloudflare, as page views for that page.

Divide one by the other and you have your conversion rate — the most useful number your site will ever give you. That's also why the analytics snippet needs to be on the form page.

Keep that "how did you hear about me?" field. It's the only way to tell which of your efforts is actually bringing people in, and it costs a single dropdown.

Optional — live data on a static site

Sooner or later you'll want something your HTML can't do alone: a live badge, a follower count, anything that talks to another service's API. You can't do that from a static page, because it would mean putting your secret API key in public HTML.

The fix is a small serverless function — Cloudflare Workers has a free tier. Your key stays on the Worker, your page just asks the Worker for the answer.

The gotcha that gets everybody: a Worker only answers requests from web addresses on its allowlist — that's CORS. Hard-code your domain in there and everything works fine, until the day you change domains. Then your page silently breaks with a CORS error in the browser console while looking perfectly healthy from the outside. Change your domain, update the allowlist, redeploy — same sitting, every time. Keep the old address in the list during the switchover.

When it breaks — the checklist

My domain shows a 404, or the registrar's parking page

Usually one of three things. Check in this order:

  1. Did you delete the registrar's original A and www records? Leftovers compete with GitHub's and you get an intermittent parking page.
  2. Has DNS finished propagating? Give it a couple of hours before assuming it's broken.
  3. Is the custom domain saved in Settings → Pages, with a green tick?

If GitHub shows a green tick and you still get 404s on some pages but not others, it's not DNS at all — see the .nojekyll entry below.

Some pages 404 even though the site works

GitHub Pages runs a tool called Jekyll over your files by default, and on a plain HTML site it can interfere with how folder addresses resolve — so /mypage/ 404s while /index.html is fine.

The fix is an empty file named exactly .nojekyll in your repo root. That switches Jekyll off. Note the leading dot, and note there's no file extension.

Once it's there, leave it there. If 404s return after you reorganise files, check it's still present before anything else.

My images have an ugly white border

Almost always a transparency problem, not a CSS one. JPEG cannot store transparency. If you export a PNG that has transparent space around the edges as a .jpg, every transparent pixel turns white.

The tell-tale sign is that the border is uneven — slightly different thickness on each side. A border you designed would be uniform; a flattened margin isn't.

Two fixes: save as .png to keep the transparency, or flatten it onto your background colour deliberately before exporting. Either way, keep the original PNG as your master — re-exporting to JPEG will bring the halo back every single time.

My browser warns the site isn't secure

You ticked Enforce HTTPS before GitHub finished issuing the certificate. Untick it, wait — up to an hour is normal, occasionally longer — then tick it again once the option is no longer greyed out.

If it's still failing after a day, remove the custom domain, save, re-add it, and save again. That kicks off a fresh certificate request.

Analytics shows nothing
  • Check the snippet is actually on the live page — open the page, view source, search for beacon.min.js.
  • Confirm you're on the account-level Web Analytics page, not a domain's own Analytics tab. If your DNS points straight at GitHub, that tab will always be empty.
  • Give it 20–30 minutes; it isn't instant.
  • Try without an ad blocker. Blockers stop the beacon, so your own visits often won't register.
Something on my page worked yesterday and now doesn't

If you changed your domain recently, that's the first suspect. Anything hard-coded to your old address stops working — a Worker's CORS allowlist, an API service's registered domain, absolute links inside your own HTML.

Open your browser's developer console on the broken page. A CORS error names the exact service that needs its allowlist updated.

To avoid this: use relative links (/about.html, not https://yourdomain.com/about.html) so your own pages never care what the site is called.

My form doesn't send
  • Is the access key pasted in, with the quotes intact?
  • Did you confirm your email with the form service? Most won't deliver until you do.
  • Check your spam folder — the first submission often lands there. Mark it as safe.
  • Open the developer console and submit. An error there tells you whether the request left your page at all.
